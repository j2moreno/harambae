<div id="description" class="section5">Description</div><p>Glutathione S-transferases (GSTs) (EC <a href= "http://enzyme.expasy.org/EC/2.5.1.18">2.5.1.18</a>) are involved in detoxification
of  xenobiotic compounds and in the biosynthesis of important metabolites. Two
distinct  superfamilies  of  GST  isoenzymes  exist.  The  larger  superfamily
comprises cytosolic, or soluble, dimeric enzymes that are principally, but not
exclusively,   involved   in   biotransformation   of  toxic  xenobiotics  and
endobiotics.   The  other  superfamily  is  composed  of  microsomal  proteins
primarily  involved  in  arachidonic  acid  metabolism.  The  microsomal GSTs,
grouped  together  as  a  separate  entity,  are  collectively  called  MAPEG,
designating   membrane-associated   proteins  in  eicosanoid  and  glutathione
metabolism (see &lt;<a href="/PDOC00999">PDOC00999</a>&gt;) [<a title="Mannervik B. et al. [CRC Crit. Rev. Biochem. 23:283-337(1988)]" href="#ref1" onClick="highLightRef('1')">1</a>,<a title="Coles B. et al. [Crit. Rev. Biochem. Mol. Biol. 25:47-70(1990)]" href="#ref2" onClick="highLightRef('2')">2</a>,<a title="Mannervik B. et al. [Methods Enzymol. 401:1-8(2005)]" href="#ref3" onClick="highLightRef('3')">3</a>,<a title="Pettersson P.L. et al. [Methods Enzymol. 401:147-161(2005)]" href="#ref4" onClick="highLightRef('4')">4</a>].
</p>
<p>Soluble  GSTs  activate  glutathione  (GSH)  to  GS-.  In  many  GSTs, this is
accomplished  by  a  Tyr  at  H-bonding distance from the sulfur of GSH. These
enzymes  catalyze nucleophilic attack by reduced glutathione (GSH) on nonpolar
compounds that contain an electrophilic carbon, nitrogen, or sulphur atom [<a title="Mannervik B. et al. [Methods Enzymol. 401:1-8(2005)]" href="#ref3" onClick="highLightRef('3')">3</a>].
</p>
<p>Soluble  glutathione  S-transferases  are mostly homodimers, with each monomer
folding into two distinct domains, an N-terminal thioredoxin-like domain and a
C-terminal  domain  (see &lt;PDB:<a href="/cgi-bin/pdb/pdb_structure_viewer.cgi?pdb=1GSS&amp;ps=PS50405&amp;extract=1">1GSS</a>&gt;) [<a title="Le Trong I. et al. [Proteins 48:618-627(2002)]" href="#ref5" onClick="highLightRef('5')">5</a>]. The N-terminal domain participate in
binding  the  glutathione  moiety  via  its  thioredoxin-like domain while the
C-terminal domain contains several hydrophobic &alpha;-helices that specifically
bind hydrophobic substrates.
</p>
<p>To  recognize soluble glutathione S-transferase we developed two profiles, one
that  covers  the  whole  N-terminal  domain  and  one  that  spans the entire
C-terminal domain.
</p>
Last update:
<p> December 2007 / First entry.
</p>
