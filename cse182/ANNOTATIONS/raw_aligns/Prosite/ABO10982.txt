<div id="description" class="section5">Description</div><p>Glutaredoxin [<a title="Gleason F.K. et al. [FEMS Microbiol. Rev. 4:271-297(1988)]" href="#ref1" onClick="highLightRef('1')">1</a>,<a title="Holmgren A. [Biochem. Soc. Trans. 16:95-96(1988)]" href="#ref2" onClick="highLightRef('2')">2</a>,<a title="Holmgren A. [J. Biol. Chem. 264:13963-13966(1989)]" href="#ref3" onClick="highLightRef('3')">3</a>],  also known as thioltransferase,  is  a small protein of
approximately one  hundred  amino-acid  residues.  It functions as an electron
carrier in  the glutathione-dependent synthesis of deoxyribonucleotides by the
enzyme  ribonucleotide  reductase.  Like  thioredoxin,  which  functions  in a
similar way, glutaredoxin possesses an active center disulfide bond. It exists
in either  a  reduced  or an oxidized form where the two cysteine residues are
linked in an intramolecular disulfide bond.
</p>
<p>Glutaredoxin has  been  sequenced  in  a  variety  of species. On the basis of
extensive sequence similarity, it has  been proposed [<a title="Johnson G.P. et al. [Virology 181:378-381(1991)]" href="#ref4" onClick="highLightRef('4')">4</a>] that vaccinia protein
O2L is  most  probably a glutaredoxin. Finally, it must be noted that phage T4
thioredoxin seems also to be evolutionary related.
</p>
<p>The  pattern is directed against the 2 cysteines of the redox active bonds. We
also developed a profile that covers the whole glutaredoxin domain.
</p>
Note:
<p> In position 5 of the pattern, all glutaredoxin sequences have Pro while
T4 thioredoxin has Val.
</p>
Last update:
<p> December 2007 / Text revised and profile added.
</p>
